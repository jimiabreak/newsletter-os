## Draft

# Why Your Portfolio Won't Get You Clients

You spent weeks on that case study. Perfect screenshots. Detailed process. Thoughtful reflection.

And it's sitting there. Doing nothing.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: portfolios are table stakes. Every designer has one. The ones landing clients aren't winning because of prettier case studies.

## The Real Problem

Portfolios show what you did. They don't show what you can do for someone specific.

A restaurant owner doesn't care about your "user-centered design process." They care about whether you can make their place look as good as it tastes. Whether you get their vibe. Whether you'll make the project easy.

Your portfolio can't answer those questions.

## What Works Instead

The designers I see booking consistent work do three things:

**1. They show up where clients are**

Not Dribbble. Not Behance. The places their actual clients hang out. Local business groups. Industry Slack channels. LinkedIn, but talking about business problems, not design trends.

**2. They lead with problems, not pixels**

"I help new restaurants look established from day one" beats "UI/UX designer with 8 years experience" every time. Clients don't buy design. They buy outcomes.

**3. They make the first step stupid easy**

Not "let's hop on a call to discuss your needs." More like "send me your Instagram and I'll tell you three things I'd change." Low commitment. High value.

## Your Next Move

Pick one platform where your ideal clients actually spend time. Show up there for 30 days. Talk about their problems, not your process.

Your portfolio is the backup. The conversation is the closer.

## What Makes This Good

- **Hook**: Starts with relatable frustration, then delivers unexpected truth
- **Specific examples**: Restaurant owner, Instagram audit offer
- **Actionable**: Clear 30-day challenge at the end
- **Voice**: Direct, confident, no fluff
- **Format**: Scannable sections, short paragraphs
- **Word count**: ~400 words (efficient)
- **No AI patterns**: No "dive in," no "in conclusion," no corporate speak
